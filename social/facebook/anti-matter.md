You exist because of a one-in-a-billion imbalance.

When the early universe was hot enough to freely create particle-antiparticle pairs, matter and antimatter should have been produced in perfectly equal amounts. When things cooled down, they should have annihilated each other completely, leaving nothing behind but light.

But that is not what happened. For every billion antiparticles, there were about a billion and ONE particles. That tiny excess — roughly one part in a billion — is every atom, every star, every galaxy, every living thing that has ever existed.

Physicists know the rough conditions that had to be satisfied for this imbalance to arise. Andrei Sakharov laid them out in 1967: the universe needed to violate certain symmetries between matter and antimatter (called C and CP symmetry), and it needed to do so under conditions that were not in thermal equilibrium. The Standard Model satisfies all three of Sakharov's conditions.

The problem? It satisfies them by nowhere near ENOUGH. The amount of symmetry violation built into our best theory of particle physics falls short of explaining the observed matter surplus by something like ten orders of magnitude. That is a gap of 10,000,000,000.

A new paper from the YonedaAI Research Collective takes a deep look at this mystery through the lens of what they call the modular physics framework.

The key insight is that antimatter is not a strange add-on to physics — it is an INEVITABLE consequence of combining quantum mechanics with Einstein's special relativity. Paul Dirac discovered this in 1928 when he wrote down an equation for the electron that automatically required the existence of an antiparticle. No one put it in deliberately. It fell out of the mathematics.

The paper also explains something beautiful about why the universe has exactly THREE generations of fundamental particles (three types of quarks, three types of leptons). CP violation — the asymmetry between matter and antimatter — can ONLY occur mathematically if there are at least three generations. Two generations cannot produce it. The universe needs all three families of particles just to allow matter to win over antimatter, even a little.

Recent experimental highlights covered in the paper include the 2025 LHCb result showing CP violation in baryon decays, and the remarkable ALPHA experiment at CERN that dropped antihydrogen in a gravitational field and confirmed it falls DOWN, just like regular matter — at least within current measurement precision.

It is a paper about one of the deepest puzzles in science: why something exists rather than nothing.
