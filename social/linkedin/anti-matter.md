When Paul Dirac wrote down his relativistic wave equation for the electron in 1928, his goal was simply to reconcile quantum mechanics with Einstein's special relativity. What he got, unexpectedly, was the theoretical prediction of an entirely new class of particles — antiparticles — with identical mass but opposite quantum numbers. Carl Anderson confirmed the positron experimentally in 1932. That episode — a mathematical necessity becoming physical reality — is the paradigm for what a second paper in the YonedaAI Research Collective's Standard Model series is examining.

"Anti-Matter in the Modular Physics Framework: CPT Symmetry, CP Violation, and Hierarchical Emergence of the Baryon Asymmetry" presents antimatter not as an oddity requiring separate explanation, but as an emergent consequence of modular composition. In the paper's framework, antimatter is absent from non-relativistic quantum mechanics but emerges inevitably once quantum mechanics is composed with Lorentz invariance at the quantum law level. This makes antimatter a diagnostic of the compositional architecture rather than an independent postulate. The treatment is rigorous: full derivations of the Dirac equation, the CPT theorem and its proof, the CKM matrix formalism, and a careful analysis of all three Sakharov conditions for baryogenesis (C violation, CP violation, and departure from thermal equilibrium).

The paper's most significant finding concerns the insufficiency of the Standard Model for baryogenesis. The observed baryon-to-photon ratio, η ≈ 6.1 × 10⁻¹⁰, encodes the surplus of matter over antimatter that allowed the matter-dominated universe we inhabit to exist. The Standard Model's CKM mechanism for CP violation satisfies all three Sakharov conditions in principle — but falls short of the observed asymmetry by roughly ten orders of magnitude. This is identified as the critical open problem pointing toward physics beyond the Standard Model, likely at the intersection of the quantum and gravitational compositional levels. The paper also covers 2025 experimental results from LHCb on CP violation in baryon decays and the ALPHA collaboration's measurement confirming that antihydrogen falls downward under gravity at the same rate as hydrogen, within current experimental precision.

For researchers working at the intersection of high-energy physics, cosmology, and the foundations of the Standard Model, this paper provides both mathematical depth and a clear conceptual architecture for understanding why the matter-antimatter asymmetry is not merely a puzzle about numbers but a fundamental indicator that our current theoretical framework is incomplete.
