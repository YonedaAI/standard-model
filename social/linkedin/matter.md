Most physicists learn the Standard Model as a single, largely complete theory. A new paper from the YonedaAI Research Collective argues there is a more illuminating way to see it: as a modular composition of physical laws, where each layer of composition produces emergent phenomena that simply do not exist in the layers below.

The paper — "Matter in the Standard Model: A Modular Composition of Fundamental Particles, Gauge Interactions, and Emergent Mass" — provides a rigorous treatment of the SM matter sector organized around four composable laws: Law I (Size-Aware, establishing the electroweak scale v ≈ 246 GeV and the QCD scale ΛQCD ≈ 200 MeV), Law II (Thermal, governing phase structure and cosmological freeze-out), Law III (Quantum, the full SU(3)×SU(2)×U(1) gauge field theory), and Law IV (Gravitational, the still-open frontier). The key structural insight is that composing the quantum description of quarks with SU(3) gauge symmetry produces asymptotic freedom and confinement — properties that exist in neither free quarks nor pure gauge theory alone. Approximately 99% of the mass of visible matter arises not from the Higgs field directly, but from this QCD binding energy, making hadronic mass a genuinely emergent property of the compositional architecture.

The paper proves three formal theorems of lasting significance: that anomaly cancellation requires quarks and leptons to appear in precisely matched sets within each generation, that the spin-statistics connection follows from the representation theory of the Lorentz group, and — most strikingly — that CP violation in the weak sector requires a minimum of three fermion generations. This provides a deep theoretical explanation for why the universe contains exactly the generational structure we observe. The treatment includes full Lagrangian derivations, the Higgs mechanism with spontaneous symmetry breaking, QCD including chiral symmetry breaking and the quark-gluon plasma, electroweak precision observables, and a discussion of open problems including the hierarchy problem (the 17-order-of-magnitude gap between the electroweak and Planck scales) and the strong CP problem. Accompanying Haskell code provides computational verification of the key algebraic results.

This work is the first in a four-paper series treating the Standard Model as a compositional structure. It provides a foundation for researchers in high-energy physics, mathematical physics, and the foundations of quantum field theory who want to understand not just what the Standard Model predicts, but why its structural features are necessary consequences of the modular composition.
